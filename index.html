Competetive Analysis: Portfolios

Site Name: Frog
Screendraft:
Site URL: www.frogdesign.com
Date: 19.10.2012
Layout: Five boxes. Above, we see a implementation of the five box layout. The primary graphic rotates and displays news relevant to Frog's interest area. Clicking on one of the boxes will bring the visitor to the dedicated page where you can find out more information.Simple, effective and attractive.
Brand: Frog Design's web page gives the visitors an impression that they are playful and fresh-thinking, but a serious business as well. Their web sites are clean and tidy, with big fonts and big pictures.
Functionality: The top header gives the visitors choices to scope through either their work, services or letting you know how to contact them, amongst others. On the "Work" page, they present three projects in bigger frames at the top, with project titles in bright colors over the images informing the visitor what this is. If you scroll down, earlier projects are presented in smaller frames, with easy readable "footers" at the bottom. At the "Contact" pages, you can choose which office you wish to contact, sorted geographically.
Interactivity: On their home page, Frog Design uses automatic photo slideshow of ongoing bigger projects as well as news relevant to their business, where the visitor may click forward or backward. They do not use any colorchancing or highlightning for indication that you may enter the different pages, but the mousepointer changes to a link pointer as it hovers over hyperlinks or hyperimages. ightbox and if so how elegantly? Do they allow sorting, categorizing (again this is like navigation).
Navigation The navigation on Frog's pages are rather intuitive, showing the different areas of the web page on the top right. As you enter e.g. "Contact", the type changes to bold, indicating that you are to be found on this page. If you click on either of the cities presented under "Contact", the tab is highlighted, indicating that you are to be found on the chosen city.


Site Name: Teams Design
Screendraft:
Site URL: http://www.teamsdesign.com/
Date: 19.10.2012
Layout: Featured Graphic.
Brand: It seems that the approach Team Design want with this home page is too look like a worldwide, multitalented firm within industrial design. However, when entering the Europe and Asia-pages, these pages looks quite unfinished. The USA pages, however, are more finished. Usage of black and yeallow details may perhaps be an effort to appeal to the industry business. This may not help, as he overall image of the company fades, as the visitor most likely visited the Asia and Europe section first, if the visitor has navigated through the links in reading direction. They can't keep up visually with the very first home page
Screendraft, USA:
Functionality: Teams Design's user friendliness of the pages under the sectiongs "Europe" and "Asia" are fairly poor, giving the visitor a bad impression. The structure is chaotic and with too little air between boxes. This makes it more frustrating to figure what's what and where to go, and it looks is in general esthetically not appealing. At the USA-homepage, however, they have done a little more effort. As my guess is that this is the main pages as this point of the page development, I will mainly use the USA-pages for the comparison. At the top, you may choose between "teams in action", "news", "contact" or "careers". At the same time, on the left side, you may find "brands", "categories", "case studies", "specialties" and "about us". These two menus are conflicting, and confuses the visitor. If I would like to investigate more of their work, I would not be immediately sure where to go. This makes it an confusing and inefficient page. If I were to contact this firm, I would also be a bit unsure if "contact" would be for the american office, or if I would be directed to an international office.
Interactivity: On the USA front page, the main graphic rotates, displaying work done by Teams Design. Mousepointer changes to link pointer when hovering over the slideshow.
Navigation Navigation around these pages are frankly quite confusing. Clicking images in the slideshow does not take you to the specific project, but rather to sublevels of the menu to the left. Making the links bold, providing some kind of breadcrumbs, does not help the visitor understanding the page as it was not the path you went to get there.


Site Name: Seymour Powell - Design and Innovation Consultants
Screendraft:
Site URL http://www.seymourpowell.com/
Date: 19.10.2012
Layout: Featured photo + fixed sidebar.
Brand: Seymour Powell Consultants seems to want to look professional, versatile and future-thinking. The slideshow shows different projects, both bigger as well as smaller. This page is also an example of use of glossyness under the product, which insinuates that they are leaning towards an apple-approach of presenting their products. However, the simplicity of the page's looks might make the company look more novice and smaller than it in reality is.
Screendraft, secondary page:
Functionality In the very first page, and intuitive way of finding out where to contact, other than hoping for that you'll find it as you enter the side. When entering the HTML-version, you find a overview over project, as well as a fixed sidebar, making the visit more efficient. Something that makes this site less efficient though, is that there is no information concerning the projects visible unless you hold the mouse pointer over them. However, the simplicity of the side bar hides a lot of information that the visitor may not be aware of unless he or she clicks around, making it hard to find.
Interactivity In the welcoming page, there is no interaction with the slide show, which would have been preferrable. At the secondary page, however, there is more interactivity.
Navigation First thing you have to do, is to choose whether you'd like to go to the flash-based page or the HTML-version. On the secondary page, the fikes bar makes it easy for the visitor to keep the visit under control, and navigate more easily.
